Video Lecture 18 "Legal and Accounting Basics for Startups"

Dec 17, 2017  

There’s a lot that goes behind the scenes in running a startup. Getting the legal, finance (equity allocation, vesting), accounting, and other overhead right will save you a lot of pain in the long run. Kirsty Nathoo, CFO at Y Combinator, and Carolynn Levy, General Counsel at Y Combinator, cover these very important topics in this lecture.

Good afternoon! As you probably guessed I am going to review another video lecture about how to start a startup. This time the guest lecturers are Kirsty Nathoo and Carolynn Levy.

Founders don’t need to know the mechanics of starting a startup - Paul Graham -

Accounting to Carolynn, Graham meant that founders don’t need to know everything in detail, and they are not going to go into much details in this lecture.

Let’s do here what they did in this lecture and not go into much details in this review. So I am going to just point out my tookouts from this lecture.

Where to form?

Delaware - it is in the business of forming corporations and it is easy to start there. Another good point s that investors are very comfortable in investing in Delaware corporations.

How to set up?

Just fax two pieces of paper to Delaware and say that you want to found a corporations. That’s all you need to set the shell of your company. Then come documents and documents and documents, you need to choose CEO, President, etc.

What about the equity?

If the stock of your company is high, then it’s time to think about how will you divide the equity. For that, at first you need to speak with co-founders.

The execution is far more important than the idea. Hence, a co-founder doesn’t deserve more equity just because he was the one who came p with the idea.

In most of cases it is better to divide the stock equally between co-founders.

All founders must be 100% in the startup.

Vesting?

Vesting is when you get full ownership of the stock for some period of time.If a person leaves before the vesting period ends, the company can get that shares back.

In Silicon Valley the so called standard vesting period is four years with a one year cliff.

Raise money: What if someone agrees to invest?

Pay attention on how much of your equity you are giving to investors. Keep in mind that in future there may be better investors, and giving 40% of the company just at the beginning is not wise.

I raised the money. Then what?

This is when you start to incurring business expenses: costs to carry out your business. That includes the salary of the employees, office rent, hosting costs, etc.

You need to pay

All the employees, as well as founder must be paid, at least the minimum wage. And no, just the equity is not enough.
